Reddit Question

How Reasonable Is a 7% Return Assumption Over a 15 Year Retirement Horizon?

A 7% annual return assumption is commonly used in retirement planning.

One way to think about the question is whether 7% was achievable historically. Another way is to ask how often it was achieved. A third way is to ask what level of confidence is required before an assumption feels reasonable.

For some investors, an assumption may be reasonable if it occurred in roughly half of historical periods. Others may want it to occur in three quarters of periods before building a retirement plan around it.

Historical Outcomes

To explore this question, I analyzed every rolling 15-year period available in the historical dataset using a 100% S&P 500 portfolio.

Rather than focusing on a single average return, the chart below shows the full distribution of historical outcomes experienced by investors who started in different years.

  • Worst outcome: -0.5% real annualized return
  • 25th percentile: +3.2%
  • Median outcome: +7.4%
  • 75th percentile: +10.5%
  • Best outcome: +15.1%

Half of all historical 15-year periods produced real returns above 7.4% per year.

However, one quarter of historical periods produced less than 3.2% per year.

The more interesting observation may be that the answer depends on what "reasonable" means to you.

If you are comfortable with an assumption that was achieved in roughly half of historical periods, then 7% appears broadly consistent with the historical record.

If you prefer an assumption that was achieved in at least 75% of historical periods, a figure closer to 3.2% may be more appropriate.

Fifteen years is long enough for compounding to matter, but still short enough that starting year had a meaningful impact on outcomes.

The spread between good and bad historical periods remained surprisingly wide.

Key Takeaways

  • A 7% real return assumption was achieved in roughly half of historical 15-year periods.
  • Historical outcomes varied substantially depending on starting year, with returns ranging from -0.5% to +15.1% per year.
  • Whether 7% is a reasonable assumption depends largely on how much uncertainty you are willing to accept.

Explore Your Own Scenarios

Historical data can help illuminate trade-offs, but it cannot determine which outcome an investor should prefer.

This analysis looked only at a 100% S&P 500 portfolio. Different conclusions may emerge when looking at portfolios that include bonds, treasury bills, gold or other asset allocations.

Some investors are willing to accept a lower probability of success in exchange for higher long-term upside. Others prefer to sacrifice some upside in order to improve outcomes in less favorable historical periods.
