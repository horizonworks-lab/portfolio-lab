Reddit Question

How Long Did You Need to Hold 100% Stocks Before They Started Beating 60/40 Even in Poor Historical Outcomes?

One of the most common arguments for a 60/40 portfolio is that investors receive a smoother ride and better protection against poor outcomes.

That trade-off is easy to understand over short periods. Bonds have historically reduced volatility and cushioned declines when stocks struggled.

But how long did that advantage persist?

One way to think about the question is whether investors received better downside protection from a 60/40 portfolio.

Another way is to ask whether there was a point where the higher expected return of stocks became large enough to offset that protection.

In other words: at what investment horizon did 100% stocks start outperforming 60/40 not only in the best historical outcomes, but also in the less favorable ones?

Historical Outcomes

To explore this question, I compared every rolling historical period available in the dataset using:

  • Portfolio A: 100% S&P 500
  • Portfolio B: 60% S&P 500 / 40% 10-Year U.S. Treasury Bonds

All returns are inflation-adjusted and expressed as real annualized returns.

3-Year Horizon

  • P95: 24.9% vs 16.3%
  • P75: 13.7% vs 9.5%
  • Median: 7.3% vs 5.7%
  • P25: 1.9% vs -0.2%
  • P5: -11.7% vs -6.8%

Over three years, the traditional diversification story largely held.

The 60/40 portfolio provided meaningful protection in poor historical periods. Investors gave up some upside potential, but experienced less severe downside when markets performed poorly.

10-Year Horizon

  • P95: 15.0% vs 10.8%
  • P75: 11.1% vs 8.3%
  • Median: 6.7% vs 5.2%
  • P25: 3.1% vs 3.2%
  • P5: -2.4% vs -1.6%

By ten years, the trade-off looked much smaller.

The downside advantage of 60/40 remained, but only at the extreme lower end of the distribution. Meanwhile, 100% stocks delivered higher returns across most historical outcomes.

30-Year Horizon

  • P95: 9.6% vs 7.1%
  • P75: 8.1% vs 6.3%
  • Median: 7.3% vs 5.4%
  • P25: 6.1% vs 4.0%
  • P5: 4.6% vs 3.4%

Thirty years produced a very different picture.

Not only did 100% stocks outperform 60/40 in median and favorable outcomes, they also outperformed in poor historical outcomes.

Even investors in the bottom 5% of historical 30-year periods achieved higher real returns with 100% stocks than with 60/40.

The traditional trade-off between downside protection and upside potential appeared to shrink substantially as the investment horizon increased.

One way to summarize the shift is to look at the 5th percentile outcome.

Over three years, the 60/40 portfolio improved the 5th percentile outcome from -11.7% to -6.8%.

Over thirty years, the relationship reversed. The 5th percentile outcome increased from 3.4% in the 60/40 portfolio to 4.6% in the 100% stock portfolio.

This suggests that diversification provided meaningful protection over shorter horizons, but that the long-term return advantage of stocks eventually became large enough to dominate even in poor historical outcomes.

Key Takeaways

  • Over short investment horizons, 60/40 provided meaningful protection against poor historical outcomes. The 5th percentile improved from -11.7% to -6.8% over three-year periods.
  • By ten years, most of that downside advantage had disappeared while 100% stocks continued to deliver higher returns across most outcomes.
  • By thirty years, 100% stocks outperformed 60/40 across the entire distribution, including the bottom 5% of historical outcomes (4.6% vs 3.4% real annualized returns).

Explore Your Own Scenarios

Historical data can help illuminate trade-offs, but it cannot determine which outcome an investor should prefer.

This analysis compared only two portfolios: 100% S&P 500 and a traditional 60/40 stock-bond allocation. Different conclusions may emerge when looking at other asset allocations, different bond maturities, treasury bills, gold or small-cap stocks.

Some investors may be willing to accept weaker short-term outcomes in exchange for stronger long-term results. Others may place a higher value on reducing uncertainty over shorter periods.
