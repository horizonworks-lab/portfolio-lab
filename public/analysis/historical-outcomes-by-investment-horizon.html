How Different Were Historical Outcomes Over 5, 10, 20 and 30 Year Investment Horizons?

One of the most common investing lessons is that a longer investment horizon reduces risk.

Most investors have probably heard some version of the advice: "stay invested for the long term."

But how much difference does time actually make?

One way to think about the question is whether longer holding periods increased average returns.

Another way is to ask whether longer holding periods reduced the gap between good and bad historical outcomes.

For an investor saving for a house in five years, the range of possible outcomes may matter a great deal. For an investor saving for retirement in thirty years, that same range may look very different.

Historical Outcomes

To explore this question, I analyzed every rolling historical investment period available in the dataset using a 100% S&P 500 portfolio.

Rather than focusing on a single average return, the charts below show the full distribution of inflation-adjusted outcomes across different investment horizons.

5-Year Horizon

  • Worst outcome: -10.3% real annualized return
  • 5th percentile: -6.5%
  • 25th percentile: +0.4%
  • Median outcome: +8.4%
  • 75th percentile: +11.8%
  • 95th percentile: +20.7%
  • Best outcome: +25.3%

10-Year Horizon

  • Worst outcome: -3.8% real annualized return
  • 5th percentile: -2.4%
  • 25th percentile: +3.1%
  • Median outcome: +6.7%
  • 75th percentile: +11.1%
  • 95th percentile: +15.0%
  • Best outcome: +17.9%

20-Year Horizon

  • Worst outcome: +0.6% real annualized return
  • 5th percentile: -2.0%
  • 25th percentile: +4.1%
  • Median outcome: +7.0%
  • 75th percentile: +9.2%
  • 95th percentile: +12.5%
  • Best outcome: +13.2%

30-Year Horizon

  • Worst outcome: +4.3% real annualized return
  • 5th percentile: +4.6%
  • 25th percentile: +6.1%
  • Median outcome: +7.3%
  • 75th percentile: +8.1%
  • 95th percentile: +9.6%
  • Best outcome: +10.1%

The most obvious observation is how dramatically the range of typical outcomes narrowed as the investment horizon increased.

Over five years, 90% of historical outcomes fell between -6.5% and +20.7% real annualized returns.

Over ten years, that range narrowed to -2.4% and +15.0%.

Over twenty years, it narrowed further to -2.0% and +12.5%.

By thirty years, 90% of historical outcomes fell within a relatively narrow range of +4.6% to +9.6% real annualized returns.

Interestingly, the median outcome changed much less than the range itself. Median returns ranged from 6.7% to 8.4% across all four investment horizons.

The larger effect of time was therefore not higher returns, but greater predictability.

Another observation is how quickly poor outcomes became less severe as the investment horizon increased.

Over five years, investors in the 25th percentile barely stayed ahead of inflation, earning just 0.4% real annualized returns.

Over thirty years, the 25th percentile outcome increased to 6.1% real annualized returns.

This suggests that the most important benefit of a longer investment horizon may not be maximizing returns. It may be reducing uncertainty and making outcomes less dependent on starting year.

Key Takeaways

  • The median outcome changed relatively little, ranging from 6.7% to 8.4% across all investment horizons.
  • The range containing 90% of historical outcomes narrowed from -6.5% to +20.7% over five years to +4.6% to +9.6% over thirty years.
  • The primary benefit of time was not higher returns, but greater predictability and less dependence on starting year.

Explore Your Own Scenarios

Historical data can help illuminate trade-offs, but it cannot determine which outcome an investor should prefer.

This analysis looked only at a 100% S&P 500 portfolio. Different patterns may emerge when looking at portfolios that include bonds, treasury bills, gold or other asset allocations.

Some investors may be comfortable accepting a wider range of possible outcomes in exchange for higher upside potential. Others may prefer a portfolio that narrows the range of historical outcomes, even if that comes at the cost of lower returns.
